# Patient Journey 01 -- Simple: Jordan Smith

## Document Overview

This is the medical record of a fictional patient by name of JORDAN SMITH (URN 112345, DOB 14/02/1975). He is written as a simple patient with a fairly simple past medical history and context, and he is admitted with a straight forward diagnosis. He is managed appropriately without any surprises. The overall journey from presentation to discharge is described chronologically, with representative medical documentation included alongside.

### Background

Jordan Smith is a 50-year-old carpenter living with his wife and two teenage children. He quit smoking at age 30 after a 10-pack-year history. He runs his own small carpentry business, working mostly on home renovations and flooring — often exposed to fine dust, timber particles, and adhesives, usually without respiratory protection. He's generally healthy, takes no regular medications, and has had only one prior hospital stay (for shoulder surgery eight years ago). Over the past few years, he's noticed mild breathlessness when working in dusty areas but attributed it to "getting older." He drinks socially and has no other medical history of note.

### Day 1 -- Presentation and Admission

Jordan presented to the emergency department after five days of fever, increasing breathlessness, and productive cough with yellow sputum. The last two days had been marked by fatigue, chest tightness, and light-headedness when climbing stairs. His wife noticed he was confused and passing very little urine. On arrival, he was febrile (38.7 °C), tachycardic (HR 110), and hypoxic (SpO2 88 % on room air, improving to 93 % on 3 L oxygen). He looked dehydrated and was coughing persistently. His chest X-ray showed patchy right mid- and lower-zone consolidation consistent with pneumonia. Blood tests revealed raised inflammatory markers and mild acute kidney injury (Cr 138 umol/L). His urea was elevated and urine concentrated. Viral PCR (COVID/influenza) was negative. He was admitted under the General Medicine service for IV antibiotics, IV fluids, oxygen, and further workup. The provisional diagnosis was community-acquired pneumonia with possible infective exacerbation of underlying COPD and pre-renal AKI due to dehydration.

### Day 2 -- Ongoing Hypoxia and Diagnostic Work-Up

Overnight, Jordan remained on 3 L oxygen with saturations in the low 90s. He continued to spike fevers and was coughing thick sputum. His repeat bloods showed improving renal function with fluids (Cr 125 umol/L). Given his occupational exposure to wood dust, smoking history, and wheeze on auscultation, the team considered possible underlying COPD or early interstitial lung disease (ILD) contributing to his symptoms and delayed recovery. A CT chest was requested to assess for pneumonia complications (abscess or effusion) and to look for background structural or interstitial lung changes related to his occupational exposure. Nebulisers and chest physiotherapy were continued.

### Day 3 -- CT Chest and Diagnostic Clarification

Jordan reported mild improvement in breathing but remained oxygen-dependent (2 L). The CT chest confirmed dense consolidation in the right lower lobe consistent with pneumonia. There were generalised bronchial wall thickening (supporting COPD) and patchy subpleural reticulation and early ground-glass changes in both lower lobes, which were noted as possible early interstitial lung changes. The report also described an incidental 5 mm left upper lobe pulmonary nodule, likely benign but requiring follow-up imaging in three months. These findings were discussed with Jordan and his wife. The doctors explained that while the current illness was clearly an infection, there might be underlying chronic airways and interstitial changes related to his long-term carpentry exposure, which would need further outpatient assessment with pulmonary function tests (PFTs) and respiratory clinic follow-up. His kidney function continued to normalise with fluids (Cr 112 umol/L), confirming pre-renal AKI. He was encouraged to mobilise, and physiotherapy was stepped up.

### Day 4 -- Clinical Improvement

By Day 4, Jordan was afebrile for 24 hours and maintaining oxygen saturations above 94 % on room air. He was walking around the ward without assistance. Blood cultures were negative, and sputum cultures showed mixed upper respiratory flora. He was switched to oral antibiotics and commenced on a short course of oral corticosteroids to help reduce airway inflammation. He was also given a salbutamol inhaler and taught how to use it properly. The team reviewed the CT again and arranged for outpatient PFTs (to confirm or exclude COPD or ILD) once recovered.

### Day 5 -- Stable, for Discharge

By the fifth hospital day, Jordan had made a steady recovery. He was afebrile, breathing comfortably on room air, and mobilising independently around the ward. His cough had largely settled, appetite and energy had returned, and renal function was normal. The medical team reviewed him that morning and confirmed he was clinically stable and safe for discharge. His diagnosis and follow-up plan were explained, including the need for a repeat chest X-ray in six weeks, pulmonary function testing, and CT chest surveillance for the small incidental lung nodule. Jordan and his wife were reassured about the good prognosis and advised regarding ongoing airway care, appropriate occupational precautions, and when to seek medical review. He was discharged home later that day with oral antibiotics to complete his course and a clear outpatient plan.

### Follow-Up Plan

1. GP in 1-2 weeks to ensure ongoing resolution of symptoms with completion of antibiotics and steroids and arrange for referral to occupational physician
2. Gen Med OPD review in 3 months (20/01/2026) with results of:
   - A. Repeat CT Chest -- ?nodule interval change, ?resolution of consolidation, ?evolution of ILD

   - C. At that review, consider commencing/optimising COPD medications and/or need for referral to Respiratory OPD for ongoing reviews/surveillance of nodules and/or ILD
